History of the bells at St Peter's Church, East Tytherley

The tower and bells were provided as a memorial to Queen Victoria’s Diamond Jubilee, the gift of the Dalgety family, then presumably Lords of the Manor, at a total cost of about £1,300. The ringing peal was originally installed in April 1898 as a ring of six bells, and was augmented to eight by the addition of two trebles completed on 18th April 1899. The bell frame as originally installed was designed and constructed to accommodate the eight bells, indeed quotations for the two additional bells were sought prior to the completion of the six.

John Taylor & Co. of Loughborough cast all the bells, to a modern design with a flat “table” top. They are all hung from their headstocks by means of bolts passing through the crown of each bell. All are fitted with an independent “crown staple” mechanism from which the clappers are suspended. In each case this bolts up through the crown of the bell and through the headstock.

All the bells were “machine” tuned at the time of manufacture, broadly to the modern “five-tone” principle adopted by John Taylor and Co. in 1896. This would have been carried out in a vertical lathe. However, the tuning marks are slight and appear confined to the mouth and lower waist of each bell, suggesting that the “partial” harmonic tones created by the reverberation of the upper waist and crown of each bell may not have been fully tuned to modem standards. Information from John Taylor (Bellfounders) Ltd indicates that after tuning the “Prime” note of the tenor bell is F#, resonating at a frequency of 734 Hz which is 14 cents (a cent being one, one hundredths of a semitone) flat of F# standard pitch.

The bells are hung from wooden headstocks carried on plain bearings. Seven of the bells are in a cast iron “H pattern” frame carried on timber bearers. The remining bell, the “second” is in a timber sub-frame above the third.

The cost of the six bells including the frame and installation but excluding the cost of cartage from Dunbridge Railway Station to the church was £294. The cost of the two additional bells was £83-10-0.

Apart from the replacement of a broken clapper, the bells have only required routine maintenance, are still in good order and are regularly rung for Sunday Service and other special occasions.

Extract from the 18th Guild Report for 1897 (July 1898) - (Page 21)

“Secretary’s report.

In various parts of the Diocese we hear that new rings of bells are receiving attention. At. New Alresford the Guild duly brought round an opening peal last December, while in April the first peal was scored on the Curdridge bells. At Merrow, near Guildford, a new peal of six (tenor 13 cwt 1 qr.), be Messrs Mears and Stainbank, was dedicated by the Archdeacon of Surrey on Easter Monday. [11th April 1898] At East Tytherley (Hants.) a new ring of six (tenor 14 cwt. 1 qr.) has been erected by Messrs Taylor, but these bells have not yet been dedicated, as the tower is still incomplete. The Rev. S. F. Bourdillon, Vicar of East Tytherley, was formerly Rector of Lockerley, and during the 13 years he has spent in these two parishes he has seen a ring of six erected in each tower. Messrs Taylor, of Loughborough, are now engaged at Whitchurch. At Chale, Isle of Wight, and at Newdigate, Surrey, the bells have been rehung, while at Oakley some necessary repairs have been effected. At Twyford it is proposed to add a couple of trebles, while the tower at Crawley will be rebuilt and the bells restored, as soon as funds permit. Before many months are over it is hoped that the bells at Fordingbridge will be thoroughly overhauled, and a new frame put in.

CHARLES E. MATTHEWS,
HON. SEC”

Extract from the front page of The Bell News and Ringing Record Saturday May 6th 1899

EAST TYTHERLEY, HANTS.

Here on Saturday afternoon, April 29th, as will be seen in another column, the Winchester Diocesan Guild successfully accomplished an opening peal at the first attempt. The Vicar, the Rev. S. F. Bourdillon, is enthusiastic on the subject of bells. In the adjoining parish of Lockerley, where he was formerly Vicar for seven years, lie was largely instrumental in obtaining a handy ring of six by Taylor. In 1893 Mr. Bourdillon removed to East Tytherley, and when the question of a memorial of the Queen's Diamond Jubilee arose, it was decided to erect a tower and put therein a ring of six bells. The work was completed last year at a total cost of about £ 1,300. The new tower has been erected on the north side of the church, and its style of architecture blends harmoniously with the rest of the structure. Messrs. Taylor were again called upon to supply the new peal, and they have within the last month added a couple of trebles. Seven bells are on one level, and the 2nd is placed on girders above the iron H pattern frame. The bells have no cannons, and Messrs. Taylor have provided fittings, etc. of the latest pattern, including Sir Arthur Heywood's excellent grease boxes. The bells bear the names of members of the Dalgety family, with the exception of the treble, which has the Vicar's name on its waist, and the total cost of the bells and frame amounts to about £530. The tenor weighs 14 cwt.1qr. 5lbs.: its note is F.

Everyone was delighted with the melodious tone of these bells, and while the opening peal was in progress Mr. J. W. Taylor, Jun., Captain Dalgety, the squire and patron of the living accompanied by his wife, the Vicar, and others, listened to the excellent striking outside the church. At the conclusion of the peal the Vicar hospitably entertained the ringers at the vicarage.
